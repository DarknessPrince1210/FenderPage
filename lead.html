LEADING WITH LEAD

The Fender Lead is a historic and iconic electric guitar model produced by the renowned musical instrument manufacturer, Fender Musical Instruments Corporation. Introduced in the late 1970s, the Fender Lead represented a departure from the company's traditional designs, as it was aimed at catering to the changing musical landscape and the evolving demands of musicians during that era.



During the 1960s and early 1970s, Fender had established itself as a leading brand in the guitar industry with its iconic Stratocaster, Telecaster, and Precision Bass models. These guitars were favored by countless musicians and had a significant impact on the development of popular music. However, as the musical landscape began to shift towards more diverse styles and genres, Fender recognized the need to expand its product line to stay relevant in the market.



In response to this changing landscape, Fender launched the Lead Series in 1979, which included the Fender Lead I and Fender Lead II models. The Fender Lead Series aimed to offer guitarists a versatile instrument that could handle a wide range of musical styles, making it particularly appealing to players exploring punk, new wave, and alternative rock genres.



The Fender Lead I featured a single slanted humbucker pickup at the bridge, along with a hardtail bridge system. It also had a single volume and tone control, along with a three-way pickup selector switch. The Lead II, on the other hand, offered a more traditional Stratocaster-style design with dual single-coil pickups and a tremolo bridge system. This allowed players to achieve a broader range of tones compared to the Lead I.



Both models featured bolt-on maple necks, 22-fret rosewood fingerboards, and solid alder bodies, which contributed to their distinct tonal characteristics. The Lead Series guitars were also known for their slim and comfortable "C"-shaped neck profile, making them easy to play for guitarists of all skill levels.



Despite their unique features and design, the Fender Lead Series did not experience the same level of commercial success as some of Fender's flagship models. Consequently, production of the Lead Series was discontinued after a relatively short run, with the last guitars produced in the early 1980s.



Today, the Fender Lead Series holds a special place in the hearts of vintage guitar enthusiasts and musicians seeking a distinctive sound and retro charm. Collectors and players alike cherish these guitars for their historical significance, offering a glimpse into a pivotal time in Fender's history when the company sought to adapt to the ever-changing demands of the music industry and its consumers.
